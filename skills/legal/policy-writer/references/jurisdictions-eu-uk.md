## 0. The legal architecture (orientation)

A privacy policy is how a controller discharges the **"right to be informed"** under GDPR
**Articles 13 & 14**, governed by the transparency rules of **Article 12**, intersecting with the
**ePrivacy Directive** (cookies/tracking), **Articles 6 & 9** (lawful bases), **Articles 15–22**
(data-subject rights) and **Articles 44–49** (transfers).

Three framing facts the skill must respect:
- **A privacy notice ≠ consent.** The notice *informs*; it does not by itself create a lawful basis.
  "By using this site you consent to our privacy policy" is invalid bundling.
- **Article 13 vs 14 is the master fork.** Art 13 = data collected **directly** from the person.
  Art 14 = data obtained **indirectly** (brokers, partners, public/scraped sources, enrichment).
  Most organisations do both and need clearly separated treatment.
- **Transparency is independently enforceable.** The largest notice-related fines (WhatsApp €225M,
  Google €50M) were *transparency* failures, not data-handling breaches.

Source: Art 13 https://gdpr-info.eu/art-13-gdpr/ · Art 14 https://gdpr-info.eu/art-14-gdpr/ ·
Art 12 https://gdpr-info.eu/art-12-gdpr/

## 1. MANDATORY disclosures — full checklist (Art 13 & 14)

### 1A. Required in EVERY notice (common core — Art 13(1)/(2) and 14(1)/(2))
1. **Identity & contact details of the controller** (and its representative where applicable) — 13(1)(a)/14(1)(a)
2. **Contact details of the representative** — required where the controller is established **outside the EU/EEA** but offers goods/services to, or monitors, people in the EU (Art 27). (UK analogue: UK representative.)
3. **DPO contact details** — where a DPO is appointed (Art 37 triggers: public authority; large-scale systematic monitoring; large-scale special-category processing) — 13(1)(b)/14(1)(b)
4. **Purposes of processing** — stated *specifically*, per purpose — 13(1)(c)/14(1)(c)
5. **Lawful basis for each purpose (Art 6)** — which of the six applies to each purpose — 13(1)(c)/14(1)(c)
6. **The specific legitimate interest** — if relying on Art 6(1)(f); must NAME the interest — 13(1)(d)/14(2)(b) [Source: https://gdpr-info.eu/art-14-gdpr/, accessed 2026-07-05]
7. **Recipients or categories of recipients** — 13(1)(e)/14(1)(e)
8. **International transfers + safeguards** — third country/org, the mechanism, how to get a copy — 13(1)(f)/14(1)(f)
9. **Retention period** (or the criteria to determine it) — 13(2)(a)/14(2)(a)
10. **Data-subject rights** — access, rectification, erasure, restriction, objection, portability — 13(2)(b)/14(2)(c)
11. **Right to withdraw consent** at any time (where consent is a basis) — 13(2)(c)/14(2)(d)
12. **Right to lodge a complaint with a supervisory authority** — 13(2)(d)/14(2)(e)
13. **Existence of automated decision-making / profiling (Art 22)** + meaningful logic + significance/consequences — 13(2)(f)/14(2)(g)

### 1B. EXTRA when data is collected DIRECTLY (Art 13 only)
14. Whether providing the data is a **statutory/contractual requirement** or necessary to enter a contract, whether the person is **obliged** to provide it, and the **consequences of not** doing so — 13(2)(e)

### 1C. EXTRA when data is collected INDIRECTLY (Art 14 only)
15. **The categories of personal data** concerned — 14(1)(d)
16. **The source of the data** — including whether from **publicly accessible sources** — 14(2)(f)
17. **Timing**: provide within a reasonable period, **at the latest one month** after obtaining; or at first communication; or when first disclosed to another recipient — whichever is earliest — 14(3)
    - Exemptions (14(5)): person already has it; impossible / disproportionate effort (esp. research/archiving — publish the notice instead); legal obligation; professional-secrecy confidentiality.

### 1D. Further-processing rule (both): if processing for a NEW purpose, provide info on that purpose **before** it begins — Art 13(3)/14(4).

> **Skill rule:** generate 1–13 always; add 14 to a direct-collection section; add 15–17 to any
> indirect-collection section; if both, produce two clearly separated sub-sections or a table.

ICO right-to-be-informed checklists https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/individual-rights/the-right-to-be-informed/checklists/

## 2. Lawful bases (Art 6) and special categories (Art 9)

**Six Art 6(1) bases** (≥1 required per purpose): (a) consent · (b) contract · (c) legal obligation ·
(d) vital interests · (e) public task · (f) legitimate interests. Public authorities cannot use (f)
for their public tasks. Source: https://gdpr-info.eu/art-6-gdpr/

The policy must **map each purpose → lawful basis** (a purpose/data/basis table is regulator-friendly).
If legitimate interests: name the interest AND have a documented balancing test (LIA) on file
(EDPB Guidelines 1/2024: https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2024-10/edpb_guidelines_202401_legitimateinterest_en.pdf).

**Special-category data (Art 9)** — racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious/philosophical
beliefs, trade-union membership, genetic data, biometric data (for unique ID), health, sex life,
sexual orientation — needs an Art 6 basis **AND** an Art 9(2) condition (e.g. explicit consent;
employment/social-security law; substantial public interest; healthcare; etc.). Many member states
(and UK DPA 2018 Sch 1) require an additional condition + an "appropriate policy document".
Criminal-offence data (Art 10) needs official authority or a specific legal authorisation.

> **Skill rule:** any special-category processing → name both Art 6 basis and Art 9(2) condition,
> and flag that a DPIA / appropriate-policy-document may be needed.

## 3. Data-subject rights to explain (with how-to-exercise)
Access (Art 15), rectification (16), erasure (17), restriction (18), portability (20 — only where
basis is consent/contract AND processing is automated), object (21 — **absolute** for direct
marketing), rights re automated decisions (22), withdraw consent (7(3)), lodge complaint (77).
Source: https://gdpr-info.eu/chapter-3/

Drafting: give a concrete channel (email/form/address); state the **one-month** response window
(extendable +2 months for complex); first request **free**; identity verification may apply.

## 4. ePrivacy / cookies (and policy vs cookie policy)
- **ePrivacy Directive Art 5(3)** (not GDPR) governs storing/accessing info on a device → requires
  **prior opt-in consent** for any non-essential storage/access, regardless of whether personal data
  is involved. GDPR supplies the consent standard.
- **EDPB Guidelines 2/2023 (v2.0, Oct 2024)** read Art 5(3) broadly: cookies **plus** pixels, tracking
  URLs, IP-only tracking, fingerprinting, local storage, SDKs.
  https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2024-10/edpb_guidelines_202302_technical_scope_art_53_eprivacydirective_v2_en_0.pdf
- **Strictly-necessary** cookies are exempt; analytics & advertising are not.
- **Banner rules** (EDPB Cookie Banner Taskforce, Jan 2023): "Reject all" as easy/prominent as
  "Accept all"; no pre-ticked boxes; no dark patterns. CNIL fined Google €150M and Facebook €60M
  (Dec 2021) for rejection being harder than acceptance.
  [Source: https://www.cnil.fr/en/cookies-facebook-ireland-limited-fined-60-million-euros, accessed 2026-07-05]
  https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2023-01/edpb_20230118_report_cookie_banner_taskforce_en.pdf
- **Policy vs cookie policy:** best practice = a standalone cookie policy linked from the banner, AND
  the main privacy policy references cookies/tracking + categories + the consent mechanism and
  cross-links. Complementary, not substitutes.

## 5. Transparency & plain language (Art 12) — how it must READ
Art 12(1): **concise, transparent, intelligible, easily accessible**, in **clear and plain language**,
free of charge, *especially* for children.

WP29/EDPB Transparency Guidelines (WP260 rev.01): avoid information fatigue; use structure/headings;
information must be **concrete and definitive**, not ambiguous.

**BANNED phrasing (transparency failures — the skill must avoid):** "we *may* use your data to develop
new services / for research / for business purposes"; "we *may* share with partners / third parties /
affiliates"; vague qualifiers "may, might, possibly, such as, including but not limited to, from time
to time, where appropriate" when they hide a determinable fact.

**Layered notices** (recommended online): first layer must contain at minimum the controller's
identity, the purposes, a description of rights, and any high-impact/unexpected processing. Don't
scatter essential info across many clicks (the exact Google/CNIL and WhatsApp failings).

## 6. International transfers (post-Schrems II) — mechanisms + disclosure
Disclose WHICH mechanism applies and HOW to get a copy of the safeguards.
- **Adequacy decision (Art 45)** — adequate countries (e.g. Andorra, Argentina, **Brazil** (Art 45 adequacy adopted 27 Jan 2026), Canada (commercial), Israel, Japan, NZ, South Korea, Switzerland, UK). The **EU→UK adequacy decisions (GDPR + LED) were renewed on 18/19 Dec 2025** — after a June→Dec 2025 bridge extension (Implementing Decision (EU) 2025/1226) — and now run to **27 Dec 2031** with a joint review after 4 years. [Sources: EC IP/26/229 (Brazil, 27 Jan 2026); EC IP/25/3059 (UK renewal, 19 Dec 2025); both verified 2026-07-09]
- **EU–US Data Privacy Framework (DPF)** — transfers to US orgs **self-certified** under the DPF.
  **Upheld by the EU General Court in *Latombe* on 3 Sept 2025**; Latombe's ECJ appeal (Case C-703/25 P, brought 31 Oct 2025) is **pending** as of 2026-07-05, no hearing date announced (flag residual risk). [Source: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/PDF/?uri=OJ:C_202506610, accessed 2026-07-05] UK has a UK–US "Data Bridge" (DPF UK Extension) from 12 Oct 2023.
  Verified: https://iapp.org/news/a/european-general-court-dismisses-latombe-challenge-upholds-eu-us-data-privacy-framework · https://www.dataprivacyframework.gov/Program-Overview
- **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** — EU 2021 modular SCCs; post-Schrems II a Transfer Impact
  Assessment (TIA) must exist (not disclosed, but required).
- **UK IDTA / UK Addendum to EU SCCs** — for UK→third-country transfers (mandatory for new transfers since 21 Sept 2022).
- **Binding Corporate Rules (Art 47)** · **Art 49 derogations** (narrow/exceptional only).

> **Skill rule:** if any sub-processor/hosting is outside the EEA/UK, generate a transfers section
> naming the mechanism + a "request a copy of safeguards" line; for US recipients default to DPF only
> if the recipient is self-certified, else SCCs/IDTA; add the DPF appeal caveat.

## 7. UK deltas (UK GDPR + DPA 2018 + DUAA 2025)
- **Regulator/complaint route:** the **ICO** (ico.org.uk), not an EU DPA. (Under DUAA 2025 Part 6 the ICO — a corporation sole — is being reconstituted as a body corporate, the **Information Commission** (s.117 establishes it; s.118 abolishes the office of Information Commissioner); the transition is expected during 2026 and the ICO remains the operative regulator/complaint route until it commences. [Source: Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 c.18 Part 6, legislation.gov.uk; verified 2026-07-09])
- **UK representative:** non-UK controllers serving/monitoring UK individuals must appoint one (UK GDPR Art 27) — separate from any EU rep.
- **Child's digital age of consent: 13** in the UK (vs GDPR default 16). See §8.
- **DPA 2018** adds UK exemptions + Sch 1 conditions + the "appropriate policy document".
- **Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 (DUAA)** — received Royal Assent 19 June 2025. Key points the skill should reflect for UK policies:
  - **Complaints-handling duty in force since 19 June 2026** (commenced by SI 2026/82; controllers only, no small-business exemption): clear process to complain directly; accept complaints by any route (incl. social media); **acknowledge within 30 days**; substantive response without undue delay; and **inform individuals of the right to complain in the privacy notice**.
    Verified: https://cms.law/en/gbr/legal-updates/data-use-and-access-act-2025-new-statutory-rules-on-handling-data-protection-complaints-from-19th-june-2026 · https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2026/82/contents/made (accessed 2026-07-05)
  - "Recognised legitimate interests" (a new basis not needing a balancing test for listed cases) and liberalised automated decision-making (new Arts 22A-22D: prohibition replaced by a safeguards regime, stricter for special-category) - both in force since 5 Feb 2026 (SI 2026/82); relaxed research notices. [Source: https://ico.org.uk/about-the-ico/what-we-do/legislation-we-cover/data-use-and-access-act-2025/the-data-use-and-access-act-2025-what-does-it-mean-for-organisations/, accessed 2026-07-05]
- **EU+UK dual exposure:** need both reps, both complaint routes, both transfer regimes.

## 8. Children's digital age of consent (Art 8 — information society services)
**Default 16**; member states may lower to **13**. Country examples (verify the target country; default 16 if unknown): **13** — UK, Belgium, Denmark, Estonia, Finland, Latvia, Malta, Portugal, Sweden; **14** — Austria, Bulgaria, Cyprus, Italy, Lithuania, Spain; **15** — Czechia, France, Greece, Slovenia (ZVOP-2, in force 26 Jan 2023); **16** — Croatia, Germany, Hungary, Ireland, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Poland, Romania, Slovakia. (Lists vary slightly by source.) Below the threshold → parental consent + reasonable-efforts verification + age-appropriate language.
Sources: https://gdpr-info.eu/art-8-gdpr/ · https://euconsent.eu/digital-age-of-consent-under-the-gdpr/ (accessed 2026-07-05) · https://uk.practicallaw.thomsonreuters.com/w-021-3094

## 9. AI / automated decisions (Art 22 + EU AI Act overlap)
If processing involves **solely-automated** decisions with legal/significant effect (credit, hiring,
insurance pricing), disclose existence, **meaningful information about the logic**, and the
**significance/consequences**, plus safeguards (human review, contest). The **EU AI Act Art 50**
adds transparency duties (e.g. tell users they are interacting with a chatbot at first interaction;
mark AI-generated content) — Art 50 applies **from 2 August 2026**. [Source: https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/50/, accessed 2026-07-05] (See `platform-cookies-ai.md`.)

## 10. Enforcement examples (verified, themed to notices/transparency/consent)
Use these to make the stakes concrete; do NOT invent new ones.
- **WhatsApp — €225M** (Irish DPC, Sept 2021): Art 13/14 information not provided / not easily accessible; transparency breach.
- **Google — €50M** (CNIL, Jan 2019): transparency + invalid consent; info spread across 5–6 clicks. The benchmark transparency case.
- **CNIL cookie fines** — Google €100M / Amazon €35M (Dec 2020: advertising cookies placed without prior consent); Google €150M / Facebook €60M (Dec 2021: rejection harder than acceptance). [Source: https://www.cnil.fr/en/cookies-facebook-ireland-limited-fined-60-million-euros, accessed 2026-07-05]
- **TikTok — €345M** (Irish DPC, Sept 2023): children's data / default settings / age-transparency.
- **Meta — €1.2B** (Irish DPC, May 2023): unlawful US transfers post-Schrems II.
- **LinkedIn — €310M** (Irish DPC, Oct 2024): transparency + consent/legitimate-interest in behavioural ads.
- **Context:** **EDPB launched its 2026 Coordinated Enforcement Framework on Articles 12–14 transparency on 19 March 2026; 25 DPAs participating** — privacy notices are being actively audited.
  Verified: https://www.edpb.europa.eu/news/news/2026/cef-2026-edpb-launches-coordinated-enforcement-action-transparency-and-information_en
